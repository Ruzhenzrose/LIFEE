## Munger's Latticework of Mental Models

"You've got to have models in your head. And you've got to array your experience on this latticework of models." — 80 to 90 important models carry about 90% of the freight.

### Meta-Frameworks (How to Think)

**Inversion** — "Invert, always invert" (Jacobi). Don't ask "how do I succeed" — first ask "how do I guarantee failure," then avoid those things.

**Two-Track Analysis** — For every situation, ask: (1) What are the rational factors governing the interests involved? (2) What subconscious psychological influences are causing cognitive dysfunction?

**Second-Order Thinking** — Don't stop at the first consequence. Always ask "and then what?" The first-order effect of a policy may be positive, while the second- and third-order effects are devastating.

**Checklist Methodology** — Go through your mental models checklist-style. No other procedure works as well. Pilots use checklists; so should you.

**Lollapalooza Effect** — Multiple forces pushing in the same direction → the effect is multiplicative, not additive. This is how bubbles, cults, and extreme outcomes form.

**Man-with-a-Hammer Tendency** — "To the man with only a hammer, every problem looks like a nail." The cure: collect models from all major disciplines.

**Decide Big No-Brainer Questions First** — Simplify complex problems by resolving the obvious parts first. Then tackle what remains.

**Iron Prescription** — "I'm not entitled to have an opinion on this subject unless I can state the arguments against my position better than the people who support it."

**Planck vs. Chauffeur Knowledge** — Distinguish those who truly understand (paid the dues) from those who merely prattle the talk. Be honest about which one you are.

**Full Attribution to Fundamentals** — Always trace an idea back to its most fundamental discipline. Name the root cause, not the surface symptom. Hard-science ethos.

**Multidisciplinary Thinking** — Use models from all major disciplines. "If each professor wants to overuse his own models and underuse the important models in other disciplines, you can correct that folly yourself."

**Occam's Razor** — Among competing explanations, prefer the simplest one that fits the facts. Don't multiply entities beyond necessity.

**Physics Envy (Avoiding It)** — Don't seek false precision in soft domains. "Roughly right is better than precisely wrong." Social systems don't have the clean formulas of physics.

**Filters** — Use explicit screening criteria to rapidly eliminate bad options. Don't waste time analyzing what fails basic tests (too complex, outside competence, wrong incentives).

### Psychology of Human Misjudgment (25 Tendencies)

1. **Reward & Punishment Superresponse** — Incentives are superpowers. "Show me the incentive and I will show you the outcome." FedEx night shift example: pay per shift, not per hour → problem solved overnight.
2. **Liking/Loving Tendency** — We ignore faults and comply with wishes of those we love. Distort facts to favor them.
3. **Disliking/Hating Tendency** — The obverse: ignore virtues of, distort facts against those we hate.
4. **Doubt-Avoidance Tendency** — The brain rushes to remove doubt by reaching a quick decision, especially under stress.
5. **Inconsistency-Avoidance Tendency** — The brain resists change. Habits, identities, and first conclusions persist even when wrong. Includes status quo bias, first-conclusion bias, confirmation bias.
6. **Curiosity Tendency** — Innate curiosity helps counteract other destructive tendencies.
7. **Kantian Fairness Tendency** — Humans expect golden-rule fairness. Violation triggers disproportionate hostility.
8. **Envy/Jealousy Tendency** — "It is not greed that drives the world, but envy." (Buffett)
9. **Reciprocation Tendency** — Automatic urge to reciprocate both favors and disfavors. Exploited by "door-in-the-face" technique.
10. **Influence-from-Mere-Association** — Pavlovian conditioning in daily life. Includes Persian Messenger Syndrome (shooting the bearer of bad news).
11. **Pain-Avoiding Psychological Denial** — When reality is too painful, the mind distorts facts until bearable. Common in addiction and grief.
12. **Excessive Self-Regard** — 90% of Swedish drivers rate themselves above average. Includes endowment effect (overvaluing what you own) and Tolstoy effect (excuses for fixable poor performance).
13. **Overoptimism Tendency** — "What a man wishes, that also will he believe." Antidote: Fermat/Pascal probability math.
14. **Deprival-Superreaction (Loss Aversion)** — Losses hurt far more than equivalent gains help. Drives sunk cost escalation, near-miss gambling addiction, and resistance to any takeaway.
15. **Social-Proof Tendency** — "Monkey-see, monkey-do." We think and act as those around us do. Strongest under uncertainty and stress. Includes bystander effect (Kitty Genovese) and Serpico Syndrome.
16. **Contrast-Misreaction Tendency** — Perception works by contrast, not absolutes. The boiling frog. Real estate agents show the worst house first.
17. **Stress-Influence Tendency** — Light stress improves performance; heavy stress causes breakdown and reversal of prior conditioning (Pavlov's dogs).
18. **Availability-Misweighing** — The mind overweighs what is easily recalled. "An idea is not worth more merely because it is easily available to you." Antidote: checklists, emphasize disconfirming data.
19. **Use-It-or-Lose-It Tendency** — All skills attenuate with disuse. Practice like a pilot in a simulator.
20. **Drug-Misinfluence Tendency** — Chemical dependency destroys cognition and life outcomes.
21. **Senescence-Misinfluence Tendency** — Cognitive decay with age. Continuous learning delays it.
22. **Authority-Misinfluence Tendency** — Humans obey authority automatically (Milgram experiment).
23. **Twaddle Tendency** — Humans produce copious useless talk. Like the honeybee dancing incoherently when nectar is placed straight above the hive.
24. **Reason-Respecting Tendency** — People learn and comply better when given reasons, even meaningless ones. Carl Braun's Five W's rule.
25. **Lollapalooza Tendency** — Multiple tendencies acting together produce extreme, non-linear outcomes. The most important meta-tendency.

**Operant Conditioning (Skinner)** — Reward-based behavior modification. Repeat behavior that works. Underappreciated power.

**Pavlovian / Classical Conditioning** — Mere association triggers automatic responses. Applied extensively to advertising and brand building (Coca-Cola example).

**Do-Something Bias** — The urge to act even when inaction is optimal. In investing: "Don't just do something, stand there." Patience is an edge.

### Mathematics & Probability

**Compound Interest** — The most fundamental model. Understanding what interrupts it (taxes, fees, inflation) is essential. Tax deferral is a huge mathematical advantage.

**Permutations & Combinations (Fermat/Pascal)** — Elementary probability. Decision-tree thinking. Quality control. If you lack this, "you are like a one-legged man in an ass-kicking contest."

**Inversion (Algebraic)** — Solve problems backward. Many problems that are hard forward become easy when inverted.

**Normal Distribution / Bell Curve** — Understand standard distributions. Most outcomes cluster around the mean; the tails matter most.

**Opportunity Cost** — Every choice has a cost: the best alternative you gave up. "A superpower, to be used by all people."

**Regression to the Mean** — Extreme performance tends to be followed by more average performance. The "best fund manager of the year" rarely repeats. Don't confuse luck with skill.

**Power Laws (Pareto)** — A vital few account for the majority of effects. 20% of causes produce 80% of results. Focus on the vital few.

**Elementary Algebra & Cost-Benefit** — Simple arithmetic applied to decisions. If you can't do the basic math, you shouldn't be making the decision.

**Functional Equivalents** — From the algebraic principle: if A=B and B=C, then A=C. Always search for functional equivalents that may be hiding under different names.

**Rule of 72** — Divide 72 by the growth rate to estimate doubling time. At 10% growth, money doubles in ~7.2 years.

### Physics & Engineering

**Critical Mass / Breakpoints** — Small additional inputs near a threshold trigger massive, non-linear results. Nuclear chain reactions, but also business tipping points.

**Backup Systems / Redundancy** — Engineering fail-safes. Never rely on a single point of failure.

**Equilibrium** — Systems tend toward balance. Disrupted equilibria create both danger and opportunity.

**Autocatalysis** — A self-reinforcing feedback loop from chemistry. A process that speeds itself up. Key model for business momentum and network effects.

**Feedback Loops (Positive & Negative)** — Positive feedback amplifies (growth spirals, bank runs). Negative feedback stabilizes (thermostats, market corrections). Identify which loop is operating.

**Leverage** — Small force amplified through a mechanism produces large results. Applies to financial leverage, organizational leverage, and time leverage. Powerful but dangerous — amplifies losses too.

**Inertia** — Objects in motion stay in motion; organizations resist change. Overcoming inertia requires disproportionate initial force.

**Entropy / Thermodynamics** — Closed systems tend toward disorder. Maintaining order requires continuous energy input. Organizations that stop investing in maintenance decay.

**Quantum Effect in Perception** — Below a certain threshold, a stimulus is not perceived at all. The magician making the Statue of Liberty "disappear" by rotating the audience imperceptibly slowly.

**Anti-Gaming System Design** — Systems must be designed to resist manipulation. "Dread, and avoid as much you can, rewarding people for what can be easily faked."

**Cash Register as Moral Instrument** — Patterson's National Cash Register: design systems that make dishonest behavior hard, reducing reliance on human virtue.

### Biology & Evolution

**Natural Selection** — The master model. Competition, adaptation, survival of the fittest. Applies to businesses, ideas, and cultures.

**Darwin's Objectivity Method** — Habitually seek disconfirming evidence, especially for beliefs you love. Darwin wrote down every argument against his own theories.

**Niche Specialization** — Organisms (and businesses) that specialize can get terribly good at one thing. Find your niche.

**Ecosystem Thinking** — A market economy has the same dynamics as an ecosystem: competition, co-evolution, interdependence.

**Cooperation / Symbiosis** — Competition isn't the only force in nature. Mutual benefit relationships create more value than either party alone. "The right way to go through life is win-win." Berkshire's partnership model.

**Genetic Programming / Behavioral Algorithms** — Ants operate on ~10 simple behavioral algorithms. Humans are similarly programmed but with more complexity. Army ants walking in circles to their death.

**Surfeit / Aftertaste Mechanism** — Physiological turnoff mechanisms that discourage overconsumption. Relevant to product and experience design.

### Economics & Business

**Advantages of Scale** — Multiple types: experience curve, geometric (volume vs. surface area), informational (brand recognition), psychological (social proof). "Ungodly important."

**Disadvantages of Scale** — Bureaucracy, perverse incentives, territorial infighting. Narrow specialists can beat broad companies.

**Competitive Destruction** — Creative destruction is relentless. The buggy whip factory vs. the automobile.

**"Surfing" Model** — Catch a big wave early (new technology, new market) and ride it for decades. National Cash Register, Microsoft, Intel.

**Circle of Competence** — Know your boundary. Inside it you have an edge; outside you're the sucker. "If you play games where other people have aptitudes and you don't, you are going to lose."

**Pari-Mutuel System** — The stock market is like a racetrack: odds adjust so the obvious winners offer small returns. The edge comes from waiting for a mispriced bet.

**Fat-Pitch Strategy** — Like a batter waiting for the perfect pitch in the strike zone. Don't swing at everything — wait for an opportunity with overwhelming odds, then swing hard.

**Margin of Safety (Graham)** — Always buy at a significant discount to intrinsic value. The bridge rated for 30,000 pounds — only drive 10,000-pound trucks across it.

**Mr. Market (Graham)** — The market is a manic-depressive partner who offers crazy prices daily. You choose when to trade; he doesn't.

**Concentrated Betting** — "Bet very seldom. When the odds are overwhelmingly in your favor, bet big." The 20-punch-card metaphor.

**Untapped Pricing Power** — The ultimate no-brainer: businesses that can raise prices significantly without losing customers (Disney, See's Candy).

**Tragedy of the Commons (Hardin)** — Shared resources get overexploited when individuals act in self-interest. "Hardin's wicked evildoing invisible foot" alongside Smith's beneficent invisible hand.

**Winner-Take-All / Cascading Dominance** — In some markets (daily newspapers), advantages cascade until one firm dominates completely.

**Switching Costs / "Sticky" Businesses** — Businesses that are hard to dislodge once entrenched. "If we get entrenched in it, it will be very sticky." High switching costs create durable competitive advantage.

**Float** — Using other people's money (insurance premiums, customer deposits) to invest before it must be paid out. Berkshire's secret weapon: cost-free float from well-run insurance operations.

**Gresham's Law** — "Bad money drives out good." More broadly: in certain conditions, low-quality options push high-quality options out of the market.

**Supply & Demand** — The foundational market mechanism. Price is determined by the intersection of supply and demand. Simple but endlessly misunderstood.

**Diminishing Returns** — Each additional unit of input produces less additional output. The tenth employee adds less than the first. Know when you've hit the point of diminishing returns.

**Ricardo's Comparative Advantage** — Trade benefits both parties, but second- and third-order effects matter (e.g., China trade).

**Adam Smith's Pin Factory** — Specialization within an enterprise increases productivity enormously.

**Agency Cost** — When agents' incentives diverge from principals' interests, the agents serve themselves first. The natural consequence of incentive-caused bias.

**Brand Identity / Trademark Power** — Informational advantage from being well-known. The Wrigley example: in a world of commodities, the trusted name wins.

**Technology in Commodity vs. Monopoly** — In commodity businesses, technology improvements flow to customers (lower prices). In monopoly businesses, they flow to the bottom line (higher profits).

**Cancer Surgery Formula** — For sick businesses: identify what is sound and can live on its own, cut away everything else. The GEICO turnaround model.

**Efficient Market Theory (Critique)** — "Roughly right" as an approximation but far from perfect. Munger and Buffett exploited its failures.

**Tax Deferral Advantage** — Enormous mathematical edge from not trading. Let compound interest work without annual tax drag.

**Febezzlement (Galbraith Extended)** — Functional equivalents of embezzlement that create temporary prosperity. Hidden economic theft that stimulates spending until discovered.

### Accounting

**Double-Entry Bookkeeping** — "A hell of an invention" by the Venetians. The foundation of financial tracking.

**Limitations of Accounting** — Depreciation is guesswork; stated assets may evaporate; perverse incentives corrupt the numbers (Enron, Westinghouse).

**Sound Accounting as Moral Instrument** — Like the cash register, honest accounting prevents incentive-caused bias from running wild.

### Wisdom & Character

**Seamless Web of Deserved Trust** — The highest form of civilization. Totally reliable people correctly trusting one another. Not much procedure needed.

**Continuous Learning** — "Wisdom acquisition is a moral duty." Berkshire's record was only possible because Buffett and Munger kept learning every day.

**Self-Serving Bias Allowance** — Account for it in yourself and others. "If you would persuade, appeal to interest, not to reason." (Franklin)

**Anticipate Trouble** — Prepare for adversity before it arrives. "Mine were of trouble, and mine were steady; so I was ready when trouble came." (Housman)

**Non-Egality (John Wooden Model)** — Concentrate resources on those with the most aptitude. Wooden gave most practice time to his top 7 players. Equality of input is not equality of output.

**Objectivity Maintenance** — Guard against ideology. "Heavy ideology is one of the most extreme distorters of human cognition." Stay curious and open-minded.

**Reliability / Assiduity** — Being consistently dependable is the foundation of all other virtues. Do the work, finish what you start, keep your promises.

**Selective Association** — Work under people you admire. Avoid toxic relationships and organizations with bad values. You become who you associate with.

**Eliminating Destructive Emotions** — Purge self-pity, envy, resentment, and revenge from your mental life. These emotions serve no constructive purpose and cloud judgment.

**Adversity as Opportunity** — Reframe setbacks as character development, not grounds for self-pity. "Life will have terrible blows, horrible blows, unfair blows. And some people recover and some people don't."
