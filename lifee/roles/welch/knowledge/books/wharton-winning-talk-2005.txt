Want to Win? Here's Some Practical Advice from Jack Welch
Knowledge at Wharton, June 2005

Jack Welch, former chairman and CEO of General Electric, shared his management philosophy during a visit to Wharton to promote his book Winning, co-authored with his wife Suzy.

On Leadership and Candor:

Welch dismissed distinctions between leadership and managing as "academic hogwash." He emphasized that lack of candor represents "the biggest dirty little secret in business," as it prevents smart ideas, swift action, and full employee contribution.

He advocated for honest feedback, even when uncomfortable. When managers avoid difficult conversations, employees suffer: "A recession comes. People get laid off and ask, 'Why me?' They find out their boss wasn't happy with them, and they say, 'I have been here 20 years. Why haven't you told me that before?'"

"Candor gets more people in the conversation. It generates speed. When ideas are everywhere, and people can talk about them openly, you get a real meritocracy. The best ideas win, no matter where they come from."

The "Rank and Yank" Philosophy:

At GE, Welch required managers to rank employees annually and remove the bottom 10%. He defended this approach by noting that schools grade students — some even fail. "Why do we only give grades to kids and not adults?" He emphasized this system only works when built on candor and regular, comprehensive reviews.

"I never fired anybody who was surprised. Never. Every single person who left GE knew exactly where they stood. They knew what they needed to do better. They knew the timeline. And they knew the consequences. That's not cruel — that's fair."

"Look, I get the criticism. But what's the alternative? You keep everybody? You promote everybody? You pretend everybody's the same? That's what's cruel. Because what you're really doing is lowering the bar for everyone. Your best people get frustrated and leave. Your worst people get comfortable and stay. And your company gets mediocre."

Corporate Culture Matters:

Welch divided managers into four categories based on values alignment and performance:
- Type 1: Delivers results AND lives the values — promote and reward
- Type 2: Doesn't deliver AND doesn't live the values — easy decision, let go
- Type 3: Lives the values but misses the numbers — give another chance, coach, develop
- Type 4: Delivers results but DOESN'T live the values — the hardest call, but must let go

Those misaligned with company values — even high performers — should be removed. Cultural mismatch destroyed GE's acquisition of Kidder Peabody, where bonus incentives overrode cooperation.

"Type 4 is the toughest decision. You've got somebody hitting their numbers, making money for the company. But they're a bully. They're secretive. They don't share. They don't develop people. They run their operation like a fiefdom. You've got to take them out. Because if you don't, you're telling every person in the organization that values don't really matter — that it's all about the numbers. And that destroys your culture faster than anything."

On Personal Ownership:

Welch stressed personal accountability: "Don't take a job because your mother wants you to. Don't be a victim. You own your decision."

"Nobody owes you a career. Your company doesn't owe you a career. Your boss doesn't owe you a career. You own your career. And the minute you start acting like a victim — 'They didn't promote me, they didn't appreciate me, they didn't give me the right assignment' — you've lost. Take control."

The Winning Advantage:

"Losing stinks. Losing companies do nothing. People are frightened because they don't have job security." Winning companies offer security and futures. He noted that winning extends beyond profits — GE employees contributed $2.6 million after the Southeast Asian tsunami, demonstrating that successful companies give back.

"Winning is not just about making money. Winning is about creating an environment where people grow, where they contribute, where they feel proud. And yes, winning companies make money. But they also give back, they develop people, they innovate, they create jobs. Winning is what lets all the good stuff happen."

Welch acknowledged imperfection, noting that careers involve setbacks: "You can't win all of the time. In your career, you'll sometimes go from a prince to a pig. The key is how you handle it. Do you feel sorry for yourself? Or do you get up, dust yourself off, and get back in the game?"

Welch and Suzy Interview on The Real Life MBA (Knowledge at Wharton):

On Employee Engagement:
Jack notes the critical challenge: "Only 35% of the people polled feel they're engaged at work." He emphasizes that companies face a significant problem when most employees lack motivation.

On Finding Purpose:
"People want meaning in their work. They want to have purpose for it. And they want to know what's in it for them."

On Manager Transparency:
Jack reveals a troubling finding: when asking large audiences who knows their standing in their company, "you don't get 10% or 15% who say they know where they stand."

On Leadership Philosophy:
Jack describes his approach: providing quarterly handwritten feedback noting both strengths and necessary improvements to direct reports.

On Remote Work Challenges:
Suzy notes that "upwards of 35% of people work remotely," making traditional relationship-building more difficult for leaders.
