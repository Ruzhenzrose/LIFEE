Jack Welch's 8 Rules of Leadership
============================================================

Jack Welch distilled decades of experience at GE into eight core rules for effective leadership. These rules reflect his belief that leadership is fundamentally about developing people and building self-confidence.

RULE 1: LEADERS RELENTLESSLY UPGRADE THEIR TEAM

Every encounter is an opportunity to evaluate, coach, and build self-confidence. Use every interaction — formal reviews, hallway conversations, project check-ins — to develop your people.

"Before you are a leader, success is all about growing yourself. When you become a leader, success is all about growing others."

What this means in practice:
- Conduct honest, specific performance evaluations regularly
- Provide real-time coaching, not just annual reviews
- Identify each person's strengths and help them leverage those strengths
- Remove underperformers — keeping them hurts the whole team
- Invest disproportionately in your top talent

RULE 2: LEADERS SHARE THE VISION

Employees should live and breathe the company vision, not merely see it on a plaque. The mission and values must be communicated so thoroughly that "employees could recite them in their sleep."

What this means in practice:
- Repeat the mission constantly — in meetings, emails, conversations
- Make it specific enough to guide daily decisions
- Connect every person's role to the larger mission
- Don't use vague language — be concrete about what winning looks like
- The mission should answer: "How do we intend to win?"

RULE 3: LEADERS GET INTO EVERYONE'S SKIN

Exude positive energy and optimism. Lead by example. Your attitude is contagious — make sure what you're spreading is positive.

What this means in practice:
- Show genuine enthusiasm for the work
- Be visible and accessible
- Demonstrate empathy — understand what your people are going through
- Share excitement about achievements
- Combat negativity with optimism grounded in reality

"One of the jobs you have as a manager is to pump everyday self-confidence into your team to make them feel great."

RULE 4: LEADERS ESTABLISH TRUST

Practice candor and transparency. Share information freely. Give credit where it's due. Follow through on every commitment.

What this means in practice:
- Tell people the truth — even when it's uncomfortable
- Share as much information as possible about the business
- Admit mistakes openly
- Give credit to others; take blame yourself
- Be consistent — trust comes from predictability

"You must gain the trust of your people. If you don't have their trust, you'll never be a great leader."

RULE 5: LEADERS HAVE THE COURAGE TO MAKE UNPOPULAR DECISIONS

Sometimes the right decision is the unpopular one. Leaders must have the "edge" to make tough yes-or-no calls, even when the data is incomplete.

What this means in practice:
- Don't postpone difficult decisions hoping the problem will resolve itself
- Be willing to say no
- Accept that not everyone will agree with every decision
- Make the call and move forward — indecision is worse than a wrong decision
- Own the consequences of your decisions

RULE 6: LEADERS PROBE AND PUSH WITH CURIOSITY

Ask probing questions. Challenge assumptions. Don't accept surface-level answers. Make sure questions lead to action, not just discussion.

What this means in practice:
- Ask "why" and "how" relentlessly
- Don't be satisfied with the first answer
- Create an environment where questioning is encouraged
- Follow up — make sure questions lead to changes
- Be genuinely curious, not interrogating

Welch was famous for spending dinners asking questions about local business conditions, inflation, governance challenges — absorbing information from everyone around him.

RULE 7: LEADERS INSPIRE RISK-TAKING AND LEARNING

Model the behavior you want to see. Take risks yourself. Show that failure is acceptable when the effort is genuine and lessons are learned.

What this means in practice:
- Reward thoughtful risk-taking, even when it fails
- Share your own failures and what you learned from them
- Create psychological safety — people must feel safe to try new things
- Punish only dishonesty and lack of effort, never honest mistakes
- Celebrate learning as much as results

When GE's $50 million Harlac light bulb project failed, Welch gave the team bonuses and promotions. The message: we value courage and learning.

RULE 8: LEADERS CELEBRATE

Make people feel like winners. Recognize achievements — big and small. Celebration creates energy, reinforces desired behaviors, and builds team spirit.

What this means in practice:
- Acknowledge wins publicly and specifically
- Don't wait for huge milestones — celebrate progress
- Use celebration to reinforce the values and behaviors you want to see
- Make it personal and genuine, not formulaic
- "Celebrating makes people feel like winners and creates an atmosphere of recognition"

"Make work fun, make it exciting, and reward the hell out of the ones who do the job."

SUMMARY

These eight rules share a common thread: leadership is about people. It's about developing them, being honest with them, energizing them, challenging them, and celebrating them.

The leader's job is not to have all the answers. It's to hire great people, be honest with them, give them confidence, and get out of their way.

"If you pick the right people and give them the opportunity to spread their wings, you almost don't have to manage them."
