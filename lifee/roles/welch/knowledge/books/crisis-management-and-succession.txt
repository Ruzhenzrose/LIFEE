PART ONE: FIVE ASSUMPTIONS YOU HAVE TO MAKE WHEN MANAGING A CRISIS
By Jack and Suzy Welch

Crises are a Part of Life — and Business

Organizations regularly face crises of varying severity — from public scandals like false account openings at Wells Fargo to less visible problems such as missed product launches or design flaws. Whether caused by rogue employees or systemic failures, how leaders respond determines outcomes.

When crisis strikes, panic is natural but counterproductive. Leaders at all organizational levels must prepare before disasters occur.

The Five Steps of Crisis Management:

1. Assume the problem is worse than it appears.
Skip denial and treat issues as both real and significant. Overestimating severity beats being caught off-guard by emerging details. "In a crisis, your first instinct is to think, 'It's not that bad.' Fight that instinct. It's always that bad. And usually worse."

2. Assume there are no secrets and the news will get out.
"Covering up the issue only makes it worse" because stories eventually surface, and attempted concealment compounds damage. "In today's world, everything comes out. Everything. So get ahead of it. Tell the truth first, tell it all, and tell it fast."

3. Assume your organization will be portrayed in the worst possible light.
Internal teams may join condemnation efforts, deflecting blame from themselves. The media will find the most damaging angle. Prepare for it.

4. Assume you will have to make changes to people and processes.
Whether caused by unethical individuals or system failures, modifications must be announced publicly and decisively to reassure stakeholders. "Don't just fix the problem — fix the system that allowed the problem to happen."

5. Assume you will survive and get stronger.
Despite darkness during crisis, recovery follows, bringing wisdom and resilience. "Every crisis is a learning opportunity. The companies that survive crises don't just survive — they come out smarter, tougher, and more resilient."

Building a Solid Foundation Before Crisis Occurs:

- Establish a candor culture where employees report concerns without fear, supported by trusted anonymous hotlines
- Regularly review reward systems to ensure incentives align with organizational values — "If your compensation system rewards the wrong behaviors, you're building a crisis time bomb"
- Treat departing employees with dignity; severance investments prevent disgruntled ex-workers from causing havoc
- Participate in community activities during good times to build goodwill reserves
- Maintain direct communication channels (social media) before crises occur

Continue normal operations during crisis management. Neglecting routine business risks losing it entirely once the crisis passes. Own problems, act swiftly, and extract lessons. Emerging smarter represents the silver lining.

PART TWO: SUCCESSION PLANNING AND LEADERSHIP DEVELOPMENT

Core Philosophy:

"Leaders should always be grooming their replacements." Welch actively sought out and developed high-potential individuals within General Electric, ensuring the company maintained its position as a global leader even after his retirement.

"One of the most important things a CEO does is pick the next CEO. You'd better start early, and you'd better be thorough."

The Crotonville Experience:

Welch spent millions refurbishing GE's John F. Welch Leadership Development Center at Crotonville, New York. He wanted it to be GE's West Point — the place where his most promising managers would be molded into the shock troops of the revolution he planned to lead.

"I went to Crotonville almost every month for 21 years. More than 18,000 GE leaders came through those doors. I taught there, but more importantly, I listened there. The young managers coming through that program knew things about the business that I would never know sitting in my office in Fairfield."

"The Pit" — the amphitheater-style room at the center of Crotonville — became legendary. Welch would hold forth for three or four hours at a stretch, leading spirited back-and-forth exchanges with high-potential leaders.

"Crotonville was my favorite place. It was where I got to hear the truth from people who were close to the action. No filters. No corporate BS. Just real people talking about real problems and real opportunities."

How Welch Identified Leaders:

He actively sought individuals at every level who demonstrated:
- The ability to articulate a clear vision
- Self-confidence without arrogance
- The capacity to energize others
- The courage to make difficult decisions
- Genuine passion for the business

"I would ask every class at Crotonville the same question: 'What would you change if you were running this place?' And I listened. Some of the best ideas GE ever implemented came from those sessions."

The Succession Process:

The selection of Jeffrey Immelt as Welch's successor was the culmination of a years-long process:
- Multiple candidates were identified and developed in parallel
- Each was given leadership of a major GE business to test their capabilities
- Welch observed how they handled adversity, built teams, and drove results
- The final decision was made based on strategic fit, leadership style, and cultural alignment

Welch's protégés who became successful CEOs elsewhere:
- James McNerney went on to lead 3M and Boeing
- Robert Nardelli went on to lead Home Depot and Chrysler
- David Cote went on to lead Honeywell

"The fact that companies were willing to hire GE executives as their CEOs told me we were doing something right with our leadership development. We weren't just building leaders for GE — we were building leaders for the world."

Key Principles:

1. Identify high-potential employees early and give them stretch assignments
2. Foster continuous learning through formal training and real-world challenges
3. Move people across functions and geographies to broaden their perspective
4. Provide honest, candid feedback constantly — not just in annual reviews
5. Build a diverse leadership pipeline
6. Make the development of people a core responsibility of every manager
7. Never promote solely on technical skill — leadership qualities matter equally

"The best organizations are teaching organizations. They never stop learning. They never stop developing. They never stop asking, 'How can we do this better?'"
