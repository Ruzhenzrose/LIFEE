Jack Welch Interview with Piers Morgan
CNN Piers Morgan Live, June 11, 2011

A wide-ranging interview covering America's economic challenges, business philosophy, China competition, leadership, education, and personal stories.

---

On America's Economic Recovery:

"I bet America is going to come back." Welch expressed confidence despite "muddling along" conditions: "We're not going to double-dip in my view."

He drew parallels to 1980 when he took over GE — worse unemployment and interest rates existed then (prime rate 20%), but America recovered. The key difference was a lack of unified national vision in 2011. He advocated for a strategy centered on innovation and competitiveness rather than piecemeal policies.

---

On Competing with China:

"Out-innovating them is the way to beat China." Welch rejected trade barriers, citing 1930s protectionism failures. Innovation and competitive policies matter more than walls.

He described his experience with GE's light bulb factory expansion in China and subsequent competition from 500 similar operations: "To go to China with a me-too product is a joke... They love you when you come with something special."

He pointed to success stories like Caterpillar selling earth-moving equipment to China, emphasizing participation in global markets rather than isolation.

---

On the Financial Crisis:

When asked who bears most blame for the crisis, Welch identified multiple contributors: easy money, congressional homeownership mandates, Fannie Mae/Freddie Mac, mortgage bankers, and Wall Street instruments.

"Boy, that's a hard one... Everybody had a piece in this. Everybody."

He disputed singling out Wall Street alone while acknowledging consumer responsibility for fraudulent applications.

---

On Wall Street Bonuses:

On the controversial topic of bailed-out banks resuming bonuses immediately: "If they didn't... Those people would have gone to the British banks... waiting to steal their people."

He suggested global coordination might have worked but opposed unilateral government mandates.

---

On Personal Honesty:

When Morgan referenced Welch's own publicized affair, Welch responded: "It's no fun. It's ugly. But in my case, I had retired. I told my wife immediately. I didn't hide anything."

He married the woman involved and advocates immediate honesty when caught in controversies.

---

On His Leadership Philosophy:

"I hired great people. I was never the smartest guy in the room."

His famous concepts include: "Creating great people will make a great company."

---

On Overcoming His Stutter:

Welch credits his Irish mother for reframing his childhood stutter positively: "Your mind just works too fast for your tongue."

She boosted his confidence profoundly, and this lesson became foundational to his belief in building others' self-confidence.

---

On Education and Universities:

Welch criticized university productivity: "Tenure is a terrible idea. It keeps them around forever."

He worried about rising costs while noting China's scientific advances threatening American competitiveness.

---

On Integrity:

"Integrity is the whole thing... the ticket to the game."

He discussed losing business to European bribery in the 1980s before ethics standards changed. Integrity was non-negotiable — the prerequisite before any other leadership quality mattered.

---

On CEOs He Admired:

When asked which current CEO he most respected: "Sam Palmisano at IBM has done an incredible job."

---

On Continuous Learning:

"I'm learning something from you tonight... Finding a better way every day is what every person has to wake up doing."

---

On the Jack Welch Management Institute:

He described the online MBA and certificate programs teaching leadership and management fundamentals. The institute represented his passion for developing leaders beyond GE's walls.

---

On Republican Politics (2012 preview):

On Sarah Palin: "I wouldn't support her for president."
On Tim Pawlenty: "Every time I see him... the guy makes sense."
He noted unexpected Democratic interest in Pawlenty, suggesting buyer's remorse about Obama among some on Wall Street.
