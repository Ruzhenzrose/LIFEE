SO MANY LEADERS GET THIS WRONG
By Jack and Suzy Welch
==========================================================

Human resources should be the most powerful part of any organization, yet its impact is frequently negative. HR often functions as either a secretive power center or an ineffective administrative department focused on compliance rather than strategic value.

The Core Problem:

Two extremes of HR dysfunction exist:

1. The "Cloak-and-Dagger" approach: HR managers become "kingmakers," wielding hidden influence that damages organizational culture and drives talent away.

2. The bureaucratic approach: HR departments organize social events and enforce rules "that appear to have no purpose other than to bolster the bureaucracy," becoming sources of frustration.

The Solution:

Leaders must appoint HR professionals who embody a "pastor-parent" hybrid model. These individuals possess genuine business credibility, have managed operations, and understand organizational dynamics. They combine empathy with candor, earning trust through integrity and relentless honesty.

Effective HR must:
- Oversee rigorous appraisal systems with Sarbanes-Oxley-level rigor
- Create motivational mechanisms including compensation, recognition, and training
- Address challenging relationships (unions, underperformers, problematic high performers)

The central argument: "people are the whole game" in business. Since leaders claim employees are their greatest asset, HR should receive equal status and resources as the CFO, making people management as professional and integral as financial management.

THE BIGGEST THIEF IN YOUR ORGANIZATION
By Jack and Suzy Welch
==========================================================

Some employees literally steal from organizations, but they're rare and easy to manage with immediate termination. The real problem is employees who steal time and energy—particularly underperformers.

Underperformers in the bottom 10 percent should receive minimal managerial attention beyond facilitating their transition elsewhere. Managers should concentrate efforts on top 20-percenters and the valuable middle 70 percent. Yet most managers get trapped in endless meetings about underperformers' excuses and failures.

The core issue is guilt. Managers delay difficult conversations, worrying about emotional fallout or regretting insufficient feedback. One CEO spent an entire summer avoiding firing Harry, a 40-year employee considered "the soul" of the company despite a decade of poor performance.

When finally confronted, Harry wasn't shocked—he was grateful for the graceful exit. The organization's response surprised the CEO even more: jubilation. People appreciated Harry's history but recognized his departure was overdue.

The CEO reflected: "As soon as he was gone, it was as if I suddenly had swaths of free time." He wished he'd acted five years earlier.

The core advice: Your managerial attention is your most valuable resource. Invest it in top performers and those with potential to reach that level.

Similarly, chronic conflict-creators who constantly disagree with everything steal organizational energy through endless meeting derailments. When pushback concentrates from one disruptive person rather than team-wide, it's time for departure. Tolerating such employees doesn't demonstrate balanced management—it reveals you're a robbery victim.

ARE LEADERS BORN OR MADE? HERE'S WHAT'S COACHABLE — AND WHAT'S DEFINITELY NOT
By Jack and Suzy Welch
==========================================================

For some people, the question of whether leaders are born or made is truly intellectual — fodder for a good classroom or dinner party debate. But for people in front-line positions to hire, promote, and fire, the question, "Who has the right stuff to lead?" definitely has more urgency. Getting the answer right can drive an organization's culture and performance to new levels. Getting it wrong can too — downwards.

So what's the answer? Of course, since we're talking about real life here, it isn't neat or simple. The facts are, some leadership traits are inborn, and they're big whoppers. They matter a lot. On the other hand, two key leadership traits can be developed with training and experience — in fact, they need to be.

Before going any farther, though, let's talk about our definition of leadership. It's comprised of five essential traits. These traits, by the way, do not include integrity, which is a requirement in any leadership position, or intelligence, which is likewise a ticket to the game in today's complex global marketplace. Nor do they include emotional maturity, another necessity. These three characteristics are baseline — they're givens.

So let's go beyond them. From our experience, the first essential trait of leadership is positive energy — the capacity to go-go-go with healthy vigor and an upbeat attitude through good times and bad.

The second is the ability to energize others, releasing their positive energy, to take any hill.

The third trait is edge — the ability to make tough calls, to say yes or no, not maybe.

The fourth trait is the talent to execute — very simply, get things done.

Fifth and finally, leaders have passion. They care deeply. They sweat; they believe.

As you may have figured, positive energy and the ability to energize are pretty hard-wired. They're basically personality. Similarly, passion feels inborn. Some people just seem to come fully loaded with intensity and curiosity; they naturally love people, life, and work. It's in them. It is them.

Edge and the ability to execute are different. New hires rarely show up with them in polished form, and even middle managers benefit from training in both. But the best teacher for these two traits is trench warfare. That's because edge and execution are largely a function of self-confidence. You can say yes or no a heck of a lot better when you've done it a bunch of times and seen how well decisiveness works. Likewise, only in real world challenges can managers truly feel the power of moving quickly, demanding accountability, and rewarding results. They can also experience how damaging it is not to execute — a mistake most effective leaders don't make twice.

So are leaders born or made? The answer (perhaps not surprisingly) is both. Your best strategy, then, is to hire for energy, the ability to energize, and passion. Go full force in training and developing edge and execution. Promote the people who have a good dose of all five traits. Always remember, though, that not everyone was meant to be a leader. But as long as you are one yourself — it's your job to find and build those who were.

GETTING THIS WRONG COULD KILL YOUR CAREER
By Jack Welch, July 2015
==========================================================

If there's one thing that's in short supply in almost every organization, at every level, it's straight talk — candor. It's business's biggest dirty little secret that in most companies, most people would rather hide or spin the truth than share it, making it hard for everyone to bring the reality of the situation to the surface and fix it.

That's human nature, of course. We all have an innate instinct that tells us from a young age to prevent awkwardness and avoid hurting feelings. Or maybe we're afraid of the very real organizational consequences of being candid in a company culture that doesn't welcome openness.

But, assuming your organization wants it, getting candor right — with your reports, your peers, and your boss — is a skill that can make or break your career. Here's how to make it work with all three.

Straight Talk With Your Team:

When it comes to candor with your direct reports, the best approach is to have quarterly reviews where you sit down and say, "Here's what you're doing well and here's what you need to do better." That way, there's no BS around it.

The word "need" is very important because people tend to listen to what they're good at and they might not hear the tougher message if you soften it too much. Now, this process doesn't have to involve long HR forms and pages of documentation. It can be as simple as a handwritten note on a little card with the two columns above.

This kind of appraisal has to be done frequently — at least twice a year. As a leader, the more you can give candid feedback, the more everybody wins. You win because you're not harboring it and becoming passive aggressive. The other person also benefits because they get what they need to improve.

We know someone who started her own company and she recently told us how she hired a good friend of hers who is now really screwing up her business. When we asked, "Have you told him?", she said, "Oh, I know I should but I haven't done it yet. I'm worried about hurting his feelings... But I'm getting really passive-aggressive because I'm so mad about it."

In this situation, everyone loses and the ending is never pretty.

The Peer-to-Peer Minefield:

Candor in peer-to-peer discussions is almost always difficult, but avoiding it is never helpful. Say you're running Division X and the other guy running Division Y is mucking up your thing in X. But, you need Division Y's political, technical, or sales support for various reasons. How can you be candid and honest without sabotaging yourself?

In this situation, friendship will carry you a long way. We have an edict — love everyone you work with. If you cross purposes with someone at work, you've got to remember that — "love everyone." Keep it in a note in your drawer. If you start to see "them" as the enemy and your teams get Balkanized, it's really important to say to yourself, "This is what I really like about this person. I'm going to assume they have the best intentions. Let's have that candid conversation but not go in as enemies."

Taking your colleagues out to lunch or going to dinner and really getting to know them is all the better. These days, you can type an email to somebody sitting in the next cubicle. But don't let technology replace relationships. One of our kids works in a company where all the employees in their group have lunch together every single day. It might seem old fashioned, but imagine how it helps the work. We are huge advocates of people being friends at work — it just changes everything.

Speaking Truth to Power:

We often hear in Q&A sessions, "I don't know where I stand. My boss never wants to talk about my performance." We always recommend never going to your boss with a confrontational stance. "I want this. I deserve that. This is where it should go."

The winning play is to come in asking for help — asking for your boss's thinking about your job relative to what his or her expectations are. Say something like, "Can we take a minute to talk about my career? I think I'm doing it alright but I'd love to get your input as to how I might do better. Am I getting there? Is there more?"

Or, if you're on different sides of an issue, you might go in saying, "Here's where I think you are... Here's where I come out on this... In the end it's your call, and I'll go either way. I just want you to have another option." Now you've given your boss a way out without directly challenging his or her authority. Something like this is the best shot you have at winning a candid discussion upward.

Candor isn't easy, but it shouldn't be harsh or blatantly direct. Coming in the "side door" in the latter two relationships will always beat a head on confrontation. Getting that right can propel your career to new heights... Getting it wrong could kill it.

SIX SECRETS TO SUCCESS AT WORK
By Jack and Suzy Welch
==========================================================

Nearly a third of American professionals feel trapped in their careers and passed over for promotions. Here are six operating principles to strengthen your career trajectory and achieve success through workplace happiness.

1. Find Where Your Passion and Skills Intersect

The concept of "Area of Destiny" represents the intersection of what you excel at uniquely and what you genuinely love doing. This territory should be where you build your professional foundation.

2. Make Your Boss Smarter

Regardless of your career level, your daily responsibility involves making your boss look good. Keep them informed about industry developments, customers, competitors, products, and technology continuously.

3. Know When to Leave a Bad Boss

"Great bosses are generous bosses." If your supervisor doesn't demonstrate genuine interest in your development and growth, you should confidently pursue other opportunities.

4. Give Yourself a Deadline

Prevent work from becoming monotonous. If you're unhappy or uncommitted, establish a timeframe for how long you'll tolerate the situation before making meaningful changes.

5. Don't Fear Failure

Taking risks involves potential failure, but you'll discover you survive it. Your resilience — how quickly you recover — reflects significantly on your character.

6. Never Stop Reinventing Yourself

Reject the concept of retirement. Continuously reinvent yourself and pursue goals you've always wanted to achieve, as this sustains your vitality throughout life.

WHAT IT REALLY TAKES TO SUCCEED
By Jack and Suzy Welch
==========================================================

The modern marketplace demands broad skill sets, yet certain core qualities remain essential for career advancement across industries. While countless experts offer conflicting advice — from extreme self-confidence to humility, positive thinking to calculated ambition — success cannot be manufactured through personality adjustments.

Instead, authenticity is foundational. As we note, "The most powerful thing you can do is, well, be real. As in not phony. As in grappling, sweating, laughing, and caring." Remote executives may appear polished and knowledgeable, but they rarely inspire teams or reach leadership's highest levels.

Beyond authenticity, essential qualities for advancement include:

Core Requirements: Intelligence, curiosity, collaboration, positive energy, decisive action, and execution capability. Importantly, professionals must balance self-confidence with humility — a combination we call "maturity."

Additional Critical Traits:
- Resilience: Successful people own failures, learn from them, and restart with renewed determination
- Foresight: The ability to anticipate unexpected market changes and customer needs develops over time through understanding competitor strategies

Organizations often pressure employees toward conformity, suppressing authenticity early in careers. However, this humanity eventually becomes an organization's most valuable asset. Genuine leaders connect authentically with teams and inspire performance.

The closing message: develop necessary skills, but remember that authenticity — already within you — should never be compromised.

HOW PROMOTABLE ARE YOU?
By Jack Welch
==========================================================

Personal growth within a company depends on two key things done consistently.

First: Over-Deliver on Results

You must consistently deliver on performance within the values your boss sets for the team. When you "hit the numbers" reliably while demonstrating the right behaviors, your boss knows you're dependable.

Second: Make Your Boss Smarter

Beyond completing assigned tasks, expand your thinking and responsibilities. Present a broader understanding of where your organization stands relative to competitors and market conditions. This gives leadership "a whole new, wider perspective on the business."

A Real Example:

Early in my career as a process engineer, I was running a small pilot plant when a New York executive visited. Rather than just updating him on the immediate project, I "painted him a bigger picture of how the plastic we had fit into the entire plastics industry" and competitive positioning. I researched thoroughly to show strengths and weaknesses compared to top competitors.

That presentation made an impression. A year later, when promotions came around, the executive remembered it. I received a significant promotion because I had provided unexpected strategic insight.

The Takeaway:

Moving up requires three things: delivering numbers, demonstrating proper behaviors, and consistently seeking bigger-picture understanding. "Your boss wins on all three."

FIVE WAYS TO CHECK YOUR HIRING BLIND SPOTS
By Jack and Suzy Welch
==========================================================

Effective hiring checklists connect directly to an organization's mission and the specific skills needed to achieve it. While most managers seek "must-have" traits like integrity and emotional intelligence components (self-regulation, self-awareness, motivation, empathy, and social skills), successful hiring requires discipline and rigor.

Five critical considerations to avoid blind spots:

IQ Over All: Intelligence matters significantly in today's business environment. "The playing field isn't level. It is tilted toward the team with the smartest people." Smart hires contribute substantially to competitive advantage.

Personality Matters: Negative personalities — glum, annoying, overbearing, or phony individuals — can damage team morale and productivity. While exceptions might exist for uniquely talented people, the bar should be extremely high before accepting someone with poor interpersonal qualities.

Drama-Free: Drama-seekers, particularly common in creative industries, create unnecessary chaos through gossip and personal intrigue. Their talent may sometimes justify the cost, but rarely.

Beware the "Swell" Factor: Distinguish between healthy self-confidence (which builds resilience) and arrogance. Candidates showing excessive self-importance warrant caution.

Face into References: Conduct thorough reference checks beyond handpicked contacts. Listen carefully to what references say and don't say. Investigate concerning patterns rather than making excuses for candidates.

The core message: rigorous, disciplined hiring processes significantly improve outcomes and reduce costly mistakes later.

MANAGING A REMOTE WORKFORCE
By Jack and Suzy Welch
==========================================================

Earlier this year, Suzy and I wrote an article discussing the benefits and challenges of working remotely. The bottom line was that while "commuting to the office in your slippers" definitely has some advantages, it also brings with it potential career limitations. Often, when key decisions are made or the team rallies around a tight deadline, remote workers aren't present. It can be a matter of "out of sight, out of mind" when deciding who gets assigned to key projects or promotions.

I'd like to explore this topic from the perspective of managing remote workers.

The Pros and Cons of a Remote Workforce:

Organizations clearly benefit from remote positions. Fewer office occupants means reduced real estate costs, and employees save time otherwise spent commuting. However, downsides exist. Remote workers tend to be less engaged with organizational culture and mission, miss relationship-building opportunities, and cannot easily seek help from coworkers or brainstorm ideas spontaneously.

My Own Experience:

The Jack Welch Management Institute operates a fully online MBA program. In just over five years, we've grown from fewer than 100 students to over 1,400 working professionals attending virtual classes from locations spanning India, Russia, and nearby areas. Our faculty — over 80 instructors — all work remotely themselves.

7 Tips for Managing a Remote Workforce:

1. Take the "randomness" out of communication. Similar to how sales teams use CRM software, we ensure every faculty member has weekly interactions — via phone, email, or video conferencing — with our Dean.

2. Plan your connections. Cannot rely on hallway interactions or spontaneous meetings. Teams should commit to weekly "huddles" using conference calls or virtual meeting tools. Plan these meetings carefully, establishing clear agenda expectations while allowing time for brainstorming.

3. Use data to drive performance. We built dashboards displaying key metrics at a glance, including Net Promoter Score for measuring customer satisfaction among our student population. Track metrics like course completion and continuation rates. Share this data transparently — team members deserve knowing how their performance compares to colleagues and where improvements are needed.

4. Find opportunities for personal interactions. Celebrate promotions, birthdays, and milestones. While congratulatory email blasts aren't equivalent to taking the team out for pizza, it's essential. Work teams, especially remote ones, don't celebrate enough. Inject fun into the workplace.

5. Set aside time and budget for in-person meetings. Despite virtual day-to-day operations, bring teams together at least annually for face-to-face meetings. Yes, costs exist, but the connections and bonds forged far outweigh expenses.

6. Build a mentor support network. As remote teams expand, individual interaction becomes challenging. Using performance data, identify your best players. Leverage them to coach and lead teams, strengthening connections and increasing your reach without adding management layers. Identify these leaders based on performance and behaviors, not seniority. You want top performers mentoring others, not those simply with longest tenure.

7. Be flexible. Remember why employees pursue remote roles — flexible hours and geography. Sometimes they'll miss meetings or reply to emails at 2:00 AM rather than standard business hours. Address problematic patterns, but planned flexibility often yields exceptional work.

The Future Outlook:

The upside far exceeds the downside in allowing flexible hours and locations. Remote work provides access to a broader pool of qualified applicants. As long as work gets completed and team members understand and address inherent challenges, the future looks bright for remote work arrangements.

YOU'RE GETTING INNOVATION ALL WRONG
By Jack and Suzy Welch
==========================================================

Most people misunderstand innovation, viewing it as a rare, monumental breakthrough associated with geniuses like Einstein, Edison, or Jobs. This perception creates fear and discourages participation.

The reality differs significantly. Innovation comprises incremental improvements that cumulatively drive meaningful change. We advocate for democratizing innovation across entire organizations rather than isolating it within specialized teams.

Core Message: "Find a better way every day." This operating principle engages every team member in continuous improvement. Examples include streamlining accounting systems, launching customer programs, or optimizing factory processes — all constitute genuine innovation.

Organizations should reward small advancements and celebrate improvements from all employees. The goal involves making innovation everyone's responsibility, not reserving it for perceived "innovators." By leveraging every mind within the company, organizations build competitive advantage through accumulated, systematic progress rather than waiting for breakthrough moments.

Innovation doesn't require genius-level thinking. It's an accessible, ongoing practice embedded in daily operations across all organizational levels.

FIVE QUESTIONS THAT MAKE STRATEGY REAL
By Jack and Suzy Welch
==========================================================

Many academics and consultants treat strategy as a complex scientific methodology. However, "strategy is a living, breathing, totally dynamic game" that is straightforward and practical rather than requiring lengthy studies or extensive reports.

Effective strategy begins with developing a significant competitive advantage through deep analysis of your business environment. Five guiding questions structure ongoing strategic discussions:

1. What Does the Playing Field Look Like Now?
Identify competitors and their market shares. Assess competitor strengths, weaknesses, products, R&D spending, and sales capabilities. Understand customers and their purchasing behaviors.

2. What Has the Competition Been Up To?
Track competitor actions over the past year. Monitor new products, technologies, or distribution channels. Watch for new market entrants.

3. What Have You Been Up To?
Review your own competitive moves. Assess acquisitions, new products, or talent changes. Identify lost competitive advantages.

4. What's Around the Corner?
Identify potential threats from competitors. Evaluate talent retention and engagement. Consider possible disruptive technologies or major deals.

5. What's Your Winning Move?
Determine how to reshape the competitive landscape. Identify ways to strengthen customer loyalty. Consider acquisitions, new products, or globalization.

Once your strategic direction is identified, success requires placing "the right people in the right jobs" and fostering a learning organization committed to continuous improvement. "Strategy is simply finding the big a-ha and setting a broad direction, putting the right people behind it" with relentless execution focus.

DEAR GRADUATE, HERE'S HOW TO GET IN THE GAME
By Jack and Suzy Welch
==========================================================

Congratulations to new graduates entering a challenging job market. We acknowledge that finding employment is difficult, with 73% of graduates ending up in jobs outside their field of study, many at minimum wage.

Rather than rehashing standard advice about resumes and networking, we offer three unconventional recommendations:

1. Find Your "Area of Destiny"

Success emerges at the intersection of two elements: what you excel at doing and what you genuinely love. We illustrate this with our daughter, a poetry major skilled at predicting Oscar winners who became a casting director in Hollywood. While this path may not guarantee immediate high pay or universal approval, working where strengths meet passion typically leads to long-term financial and emotional fulfillment.

2. Prepare Intensively for Interviews

Job interviews demand substantially more preparation than college exams. Candidates should thoroughly research the company, industry, and competitive landscape. We share an anecdote about an unprepared candidate who couldn't answer a basic question about the company's stock price. Successful interviews require demonstrating genuine knowledge and offering fresh insights about the organization.

3. Accept Parental Support

Recent graduates should welcome guidance from parents regarding professional communication, interview attire, networking, and thank-you notes. Though this may feel infantilizing, parental involvement during the job search is temporary and valuable.
