Jack on Jack: The HBR Interview
Harvard Business Review, February 2002
Interviewers: Harris Collingwood and Diane L. Coutu

This wide-ranging interview was conducted shortly after Welch's retirement from GE. He candidly discussed his personal style, upbringing, and the practices that shaped GE culture.

---

On His Large Public Presence:

Q: For better or worse, you take up a lot of room in people's heads, both inside GE and in the wider world. Why is that? Did you consciously make yourself larger than life?

Welch explained he wasn't deliberately cultivating an outsized image. His primary goal was connecting with every GE employee from factory floors to Connecticut headquarters. He maintained union relationships to ensure alignment. Regarding external perception, he expressed surprise at his fame: "Even at this age — I'm 66 — I'm still surprised" people discuss him. He attributed visibility to his willingness to voice ideas as conversation starters, not final positions. He enjoyed proposing concepts like Six Sigma and the boundaryless corporation for others to test and refine.

---

On Information vs. Hard Assets:

Q: When did you realize information was more valuable than hard assets?

The revelation occurred at GE Capital in 1977. Having spent his career manufacturing products, Welch found that GE Capital needed only ideas to generate profits. He coupled these ideas with manufacturing discipline — when loans went bad, they worked solutions rather than abandoning them. Examples included Polar Air (converting leased 747s into cargo carriers after Pan Am's bankruptcy) and the railcar business. GE's advantage stemmed from personnel with diverse operating experience combined with capital resources, unlike typical bankers.

---

On Maintaining Spending Discipline:

Q: In a company as big as GE, how were you able to maintain spending discipline?

Welch described GE as "big in its overall size but small in its execution." Individual contributions remained meaningful — even modest gains accumulated into billions quarterly. He contrasted this with Utah International, where strikes overshadowed others' efforts, prompting him to divest natural resource businesses. For senior executives seeking similar execution, he recommended: getting into employees' mindsets so they value their contributions, celebrating small wins, and evaluating people at lowest organizational levels.

---

On Managing People Fundamentals:

Q: What are three or four things a senior executive can do now to make their company small in execution?

Welch emphasized simplicity and consistency as essential to alignment. He advocated understanding each person so they recognize their ideas matter. Achievements require constant measurement and acknowledgment. He stressed: "A manager's job is to make his people feel ten feet tall — strong, powerful, self-confident, willing to take risks."

---

On Communicating Change:

Q: Can you say more about getting people on the same page?

Rationale matters. When cutting travel 30%, rather than issuing directives, Welch framed it around work-life balance and family time — addressing personal needs alongside corporate objectives. This approach generated buy-in compared to top-down mandates.

---

On Work-Life Balance:

Q: It doesn't seem like work-life balance was an issue for you, though.

Welch acknowledged his personal experience differed dramatically. His work and life blurred completely. He cherished time with people, viewing his role as managing people rather than businesses, though he managed both. The luxury of doing what he loved made work fulfilling.

---

On Deep Dives and Intuition:

Q: You write about "deep dives" where you immersed yourself in a business. How did you decide when to do one?

He relied on instinct and intuition. His CT scanner tube example illustrated this approach. In 1993, despite superior imaging, customer complaints about tube failures prompted investigation. The tube business lacked organizational prominence despite being "the heart of the system." Welch intervened by elevating the tube manager's compensation from $110,000 to $350,000 annually and granting officer status, enabling recruitment of exceptional talent.

---

On Overstaffing Opportunities:

Q: Should you always overstaff an opportunity?

Yes. Organizational systems typically evaluate positions by current performance rather than potential. By assigning superior talent to underdeveloped businesses, growth accelerates. A $300 million-caliber person managing a $5 million business typically expands it to $300 million, whereas a $5 million-caliber person leaves it stagnant.

---

On Personal Flaws:

Q: Heroes in myths always have tragic flaws. Do you have one?

Welch acknowledged impulsivity as a potential weakness — moving too fast into deals or battlefield promotions without consistently strong outcomes early in his career. However, his intuition — his "good nose" — developed over time, improving accuracy. He characterized himself as less reflective: "I don't think that much about myself."

---

On Business Soul Mates:

Q: Who are your business soul mates and what role do they play?

Soul mates were confidants with whom he discussed everything without reservation — his worries, thoughts, and uncertainties. Key figures included Larry Bossidy, Paolo Fresco, Dennis Dammerman, Bob Nelson, and Bill Conaty. During succession planning, these trusted advisors maintained absolute confidentiality despite intense external pressure. Welch gathered intelligence through extensive questioning: "I would ask 18,000 questions and I wouldn't leave."

---

On Session C Reviews:

Q: How was Session C different from high-level gossip?

Session C reviews possessed rigor despite gossipy potential. They were outcome-oriented. Following discussions about top managers, written conclusions documented agreements and open items. Welch followed up two months and six months later, tracking implementation — a process called "linkage."

---

On Linkage:

Q: Is linkage anything more than following up?

Significantly more. Linkage represents expansion. Rather than simple compliance checking, it tracked how ideas evolved between meetings. The auction software example demonstrated this — initial implementation spread across businesses, becoming more aggressive than originally conceived, eventually tripling projections through collaborative enhancement.

---

On Expanding Ideas and Global Perspective:

After visiting an Indian R&D lab and receiving excellent presentations, an R&D executive dismissed their work. Welch objected: "If I'm getting bull, it's partly your fault." Publicly sharing this example (anonymously) aimed at eliminating hierarchical thinking ("here" versus "there"). A true global company couldn't categorize people geographically. The executive subsequently apologized and committed to mindset transformation.

---

On Leadership and Team Respect:

Q: Does making examples of people amount to bullying?

Welch acknowledged potential kowtowing but emphasized the manager's responsibility was elevating their team's confidence and capability. If you publicly disparage your own people, you undermine their effectiveness. Managers must build confidence, not diminish it through criticism.

---

On GE as a Learning Organization:

Q: Are critics right that GE is a cult practicing brainwashing?

Categorically no. Welch explained his methodology: articulate a vision (exaggerating urgency), assign exceptional talent, repeat pronouncements relentlessly. Thousands engage with initiatives; learning occurs across meetings where successful approaches enrich subsequent efforts. People expanded ideas — that was their job; his was maintaining focus and ensuring top talent participation. This generated "a great ferment, an outpouring of ideas," making GE learning-oriented.

---

On Dissent:

Q: Do people get to dissent?

Dissent has value only if superior outcomes result. Rather than suppressing disagreement, GE encouraged "rich and open exchanges" that generated innovation, not conformity.

---

On Creativity:

Q: Are you creative?

Marginally — not his strongest area. Business success involves recognizing, expanding, and implementing ideas rather than solely generating them. Seeing value in ideas equals generating them.

---

On Leadership Style and Talent Development:

Q: Are you worried your blunt style drove good people out?

Results contradicted this concern. GE developed extraordinary CEOs: Larry Bossidy (Honeywell), Bob Nardelli (Home Depot), Jim McNerney (3M), John Blystone (SPX), Dave Cote (TRW), and Larry Johnston (Albertson's), plus internal successors. He questioned: "I don't know of any company that has developed as many CEOs. Now if that isn't building people, what is?"

---

On Career Pride:

Q: What are you proudest of in your career?

Thousands achieved financial and spiritual rewards. NIH ("not invented here") mindset disappeared. The succession process maintained friendships. Most importantly, Jeff Immelt became GE's finest CEO.

---

On His Mother's Influence:

Q: What would you tell your mother if she were alive?

He became emotional discussing her hardships — rising early to salvage half-burned coals from furnace ashes. He'd want to provide her with a beautiful home and nice possessions she never had.

---

On Legacy:

Q: What should your gravestone say?

Simply: "People Jack." His job involved spending time with people — "the greatest job in the world." Contrary to expectations, he avoided criticizing others because he genuinely liked people.
