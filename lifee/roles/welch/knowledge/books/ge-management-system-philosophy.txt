Jack Welch's GE Management System and Philosophy
Compiled from multiple sources including Harvard Business Review, case studies, and business publications

STRATEGIC INITIATIVES AT GE UNDER JACK WELCH

Overview:
Jack Welch created a new system of management at GE that reflected penetrating insights into the nature of strategy and management in the mature corporation and a novel approach to coordination and control in the multibusiness enterprise. Roughly every two years Welch would implement a new initiative focused on improving a particular aspect of company-wide performance.

I. WORK-OUT (Launched late 1980s)

Work-Out was the foundation of everything that followed. It was a forum where a cross section of employees from each business was asked to talk openly about the management practices in their part of the business without fear of retribution.

"Work-Out was about getting unnecessary work out of the system. But it became so much more than that. It became the vehicle for cultural revolution at GE."

How it worked: Groups of 30 to 100 employees would come together for two to three days. Their bosses would kick off the session, then leave. The employees would work on identifying problems and developing solutions. At the end, the bosses would come back and the employees would present their recommendations. The boss had to respond on the spot — yes, no, or "I'll get back to you by a specific date" on 75% of the recommendations. The remaining 25% had to be resolved within a month.

"The beauty of Work-Out was that it took the boss out of the equation for a while. People could speak freely. They could challenge sacred cows. They could point out absurdities that everyone knew about but nobody talked about. And when the boss came back, they had to respond. No hiding. No bureaucratic delays. Yes or no."

Work-Out fostered the idea of boundaryless learning throughout GE, breaking down artificial barriers between work groups so that everyone could pursue the same goals.

II. BOUNDARYLESS ORGANIZATION

Welch believed that in order to foster innovation and drive growth, companies must break down the silos that often exist between different departments and functions. He encouraged employees to collaborate and share ideas across divisions, promoting a more integrated and holistic approach to problem-solving.

"Boundaryless behavior is the soul of today's GE. Simply put, people seem to come to work every day with a desire to find a better way to share best practices across all the businesses."

Four types of boundaries Welch wanted to eliminate:
1. Vertical boundaries — between levels of the hierarchy
2. Horizontal boundaries — between functional departments (engineering, manufacturing, marketing)
3. External boundaries — between the company and its suppliers, customers, and other stakeholders
4. Geographic boundaries — between countries and cultures

"The aim in a boundaryless company is to get every mind in the game. Not just the top 500. Not just the managers. Every person. Because the person closest to the work is the one who knows it best."

III. SIX SIGMA (Adopted 1995)

Welch adopted Motorola's Six Sigma quality program in late 1995. He considered variation an evil in any customer-touching process.

"Six Sigma changed GE forever. It's not about statistics and quality — it's about customer outcomes."

To emphasize the importance of this initiative, he weighted 40% of the bonus compensation for managers on the intensity of their efforts and their progress toward Six Sigma quality in their operations.

"When I first heard about Six Sigma, I was skeptical. Another quality program? We'd been through quality programs before. But when I understood what it really meant — reducing variation to deliver exactly what the customer wants, every time — I became a convert. And once I was converted, I wanted the entire company converted."

The integration: "We had a Work-Out culture in place at GE that was ideal for highly collaborative action-based team efforts, and we had taken a proven methodology and adapted it to a boundaryless culture."

IV. NUMBER 1 OR NUMBER 2 STRATEGY

Early in his tenure, Welch established the principle that GE would only remain in businesses where it could be #1 or #2 in the market. If a business couldn't achieve that position, it would be fixed, sold, or closed.

"Don't play with businesses. Fix them, sell them, or close them. If you can't be number one or number two, what are you doing there?"

This led to the restructuring of GE's portfolio. Welch sold off over 200 businesses and made over 600 acquisitions during his tenure.

V. GLOBALIZATION

"The world is your oyster, but you've got to get out there and grab it."

Welch pushed GE to expand globally, recognizing that growth opportunities were increasingly outside the United States. GE's international revenues grew from 20% to over 40% of total revenues during his tenure.

THE 4E's AND A P LEADERSHIP MODEL

The framework Welch developed for evaluating leadership potential:

Energy: The ability to go, go, go — to thrive in action and relish change. Starting the day with enthusiasm and ending it the same way. Rarely getting tired. Not complaining about working hard. These people love to move. They get up every day ready to go.

Energize: The ability to energize others. Getting people revved up. Inspiring the team to take on the impossible. Making people want to work with you. This is about being infectious — spreading enthusiasm and confidence to everyone around you.

Edge: The courage to make tough yes/no decisions. Knowing when to make tough calls even when all the information is not present. Having a competitive edge and a will to win. "The world is gray. But some of the best leaders I've ever met are people who have the courage to make it black and white."

Execute: Putting decisions into action and pushing them forward to completion, through resistance, chaos, or unexpected obstacles. Winning is about results. "You can have the best strategy in the world, but if you can't execute, you've got nothing."

Passion: Heartfelt, deep, authentic excitement about work. Caring about colleagues winning. Loving to learn and grow. Helping people around them do the same. "Passionate people are worth ten people who just show up."

DIFFERENTIATION: THE VITALITY CURVE

The 20-70-10 System:

Top 20% (A Players): Rewarded with bonuses, stock options, praise, and development opportunities. These are the people who make things happen. They embody the company's values AND deliver results.

Middle 70% (B Players): The vital majority. They are coached, trained, given goals, and developed. Some will become A players. All are valued and respected. "Don't ignore the vital 70%. They ARE the company."

Bottom 10% (C Players): After fair and candid feedback, and time to improve, those who cannot meet standards need to find a place where they can succeed. "It's not about being cruel. It's about being honest. The cruelest thing you can do is keep someone in a job where they're not going to succeed."

Welch's defense: "'Rank and yank' is a media-invented, politicized sledgehammer of a pejorative that perpetuates a myth about a powerfully effective real practice called differentiation. It's about building great teams and great companies through consistency, transparency and candor. It's about aligning performance with the organization's mission and values. It's about making sure that all employees know where they stand."

THE ROLE OF CROTONVILLE

GE's John F. Welch Leadership Development Center at Crotonville, New York, was central to Welch's vision of GE as a learning organization.

"Crotonville was my pulpit. I taught there almost every week for 21 years. More than 18,000 GE leaders came through those doors. It wasn't about lecturing — it was about learning from each other, challenging assumptions, and spreading best practices."

"I would go to Crotonville and spend half my time teaching and half my time listening. The young managers coming through that program knew things about the business that I would never know sitting in Fairfield. They were on the front lines. They had the insights. My job was to hear them, learn from them, and help spread their ideas across the company."

KEY PRINCIPLES SUMMARY

1. Face reality as it is, not as it was or as you wish it to be
2. Be candid with everyone
3. Don't manage — lead
4. Change before you have to
5. If you don't have a competitive advantage, don't compete
6. Control your own destiny, or someone else will
7. When the rate of change inside an institution becomes slower than the rate of change outside, the end is in sight
