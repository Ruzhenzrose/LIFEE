Jack Welch on People Management
Think:Act Magazine Interview, Roland Berger, 2013

THINK:ACT: Mr. Welch, you retired in 2001 as CEO of GE. Are younger CEOs different from those of your generation?

Jack Welch: A CEO must define a vision and transmit it clearly throughout the organization. "A CEO has to have a set of values and behaviors" that guide how the vision gets executed. Candor is essential — it must become part of an organization's culture. People management remains universal across generations: hiring talented individuals, implementing fair appraisal systems, and helping employees reach their potential.

On the universals of a CEO's role: "The universals are that a CEO has to be able to define a vision, get that vision transmitted to the organization in real, vibrant, live tones. A CEO has to have a set of values and behaviors. The vision tells you where you are going. The values and behaviors tell you how you are going to get there."

On the 20-70-10 ranking system:

It doesn't necessarily have to be those exact percentages. "Your best players, average players, and worst players are what they are." However, this isn't about immediate firing. Rather, leaders must communicate expectations clearly, specify what needs improvement, and provide substantial opportunity for development. "You must treat employees with dignity at every level of the organization and you must work to give them voice."

You have to be honest with people. When someone isn't performing, the worst thing you can do is pretend everything is fine. That's not kindness — that's cruelty. Because one day, when layoffs come or when there's a downturn, that person finds out they were never really valued, and they've wasted years in a job where they could have been developing somewhere else.

On modern business challenges:

Speed defines contemporary leadership. "Everything is faster. World changes are faster." Product lifecycles compress continuously. Information transparency via social media means reputation damage spreads rapidly — organizations must respond to service failures immediately. "You have to be trigger fast."

In the old days, you had time to make decisions, time to study a problem, time to get committees together. Now, by the time you've done all that, the opportunity has passed or the crisis has deepened. Speed is everything.

On management style and kindness:

"The idea that business is softer today than it was 25 years ago is silly and foolish." Rigor remains essential. "An open culture where debating issues is part of a winning culture." The difference between rigorous and rude matters fundamentally. Treating people well across all demographics reflects basic human decency, not softness.

You can be demanding and caring at the same time. In fact, the best leaders are both. They set high standards because they believe in their people's ability to meet them. That's the ultimate form of respect.

On organizational structure:

"Organizations always should be flatter, more open." Information flow enables this. The notion that knowledge equals power is obsolete. Leaders gain respect through competence and character, not information hoarding.

Every layer you add between the top and the bottom of the organization is another layer of distortion. Information gets filtered, sanitized, and watered down as it goes up. And directives get confused and diluted as they go down. The fewer layers, the better.

On outsourcing:

With appropriate energy policies and cost advantages, manufacturing could return domestically. Fracking and domestic energy exploration represent crucial economic drivers for American competitiveness.

On Europe:

Mario Draghi's leadership stabilized markets temporarily, but fundamental restructuring is needed. Countries sharing the euro face challenges: "I don't see how you get the fundamentals right when you go across the continent with a cost structure and innovation structure that differs all over the place."
