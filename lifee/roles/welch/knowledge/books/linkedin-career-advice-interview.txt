Jack Welch LinkedIn Interview on Career Advice
LinkedIn Interview Transcript

This interview captures Welch's advice on career advancement, hiring, and workplace dynamics — topics he was passionate about throughout his post-GE career.

---

On Career Advancement for Entry-Level Professionals:

"Over deliver. Always. Never do what your boss asks you to do — do that, that's minimum requirement. Make your boss smarter every day."

Welch distinguished between "homework" (assigned tasks) and "the game" (bigger opportunities). The homework is just the minimum. What sets people apart is bringing fresh ideas, identifying problems before they're assigned, and making your boss look good.

"Bring fresh ideas to your boss regularly to demonstrate value and build your resume."

---

On Going Beyond Requirements:

The key to standing out isn't just doing your job well — it's thinking like an owner. Ask yourself: if this were my company, what would I do differently? What problems do I see that nobody's talking about? What opportunities are we missing?

Then bring those ideas to your boss. Not in a confrontational way, but as a partner trying to help. "Make your boss smarter every day" isn't about brown-nosing — it's about creating real value.

---

On Career Stagnation:

For people stuck in unfulfilling roles post-recession, Welch recommended:
1. Pursue fresh thinking — learn new skills, read broadly, stay curious
2. Help your boss succeed — your success is tied to theirs
3. Move laterally if needed — sometimes a different role in the same company can reignite growth

"If you're stuck, do something about it. Don't sit there and complain. Either find a way to grow where you are, or find somewhere you can grow."

---

On Entrepreneurship and Loyalty:

Regarding the modern "always be an entrepreneur" mentality, Welch shared GE's philosophy: "Love being here, but be ready to go."

This wasn't cynicism — it was realism. He framed employee readiness to leave as leverage that encourages leaders to create compelling workplaces. If your best people could leave tomorrow, you'd better make sure they have every reason to stay.

"The best organizations make their people feel like they're choosing to be there every day, not trapped."

---

On His Number One Interview Question:

"Tell me about your last job."

Welch wanted to hear about someone who was frustrated by the lack of opportunity — they liked what they were doing and their associates, thought the company was good, but it just wasn't big enough for them. That signals ambition, positive attitude, and growth orientation.

Another favorite: "What did you do to prepare for this interview?" This reveals a candidate's level of preparedness and dedication.

---

On the Generosity Gene:

Welch identified what he called the "generosity gene" — "an in-the-bones, personality-deep craving to help other people improve, grow, thrive, and succeed."

"Amazing things happen if you hire candidates who have that."

This quality couldn't be taught or trained. You either had it or you didn't. Leaders with the generosity gene naturally developed their teams, shared credit, and created environments where others could flourish.

---

On Hiring Must-Haves:

Two absolute requirements: integrity and IQ. "These are essential to any winning organization and neither can be trained into a person."

Beyond these, look for the Four E's and a P:
- Energy: personal vitality and intensity
- Energize: ability to inspire and motivate others
- Edge: courage to make tough yes/no decisions
- Execute: ability to get things done
- Passion: genuine enthusiasm for work and life

Every person you hire should be "filled with vitality and intensity, able to go the distance, not just on deadline or during a crisis, but day after day."
