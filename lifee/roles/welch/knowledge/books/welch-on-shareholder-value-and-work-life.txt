Jack Welch on Shareholder Value and Work-Life Balance
Compiled from interviews and speeches

PART ONE: SHAREHOLDER VALUE — "THE DUMBEST IDEA IN THE WORLD"

Background:
The birth of the shareholder value movement is commonly traced to a speech Welch gave at New York's Pierre hotel in 1981, shortly after taking the helm at GE, in which he outlined his beliefs in selling underperforming businesses and aggressively cutting costs to deliver consistent profit rises. Under his 20-year tenure, GE's market value grew from $14 billion to over $400 billion.

So when Welch told the Financial Times in March 2009 that shareholder value was "the dumbest idea in the world," the business community was shocked.

The Original Statement (Financial Times, March 12, 2009):

"On the face of it, shareholder value is the dumbest idea in the world. Shareholder value is a result, not a strategy. Your main constituencies are your employees, your customers and your products. Managers and investors should not set share price increases as their overarching goal. Short-term profits should be allied with an increase in the long-term value of a company."

The Clarification (BusinessWeek interview):

"It's obvious that strategies are what drive a business. But you would never tell your employees, 'Shareholder value is our strategy.' That's not a strategy you can touch. Increasing the value of your company in both the short and long term is an outcome of the implementation of successful strategies."

"As people struggle in the current economic climate, everything is being questioned. The context of the FT interview was about the future of capitalism. That's on everyone's mind right now, and it's a good debate to have."

What Welch Really Meant:

Shareholder value is not a strategy — it's a result. You don't wake up in the morning and tell your team, "Let's increase shareholder value today." That's meaningless. What you do is: hire great people, develop great products, take care of your customers, and run efficient operations. If you do all of those things well, shareholder value will follow.

"The idea that shareholder value is a strategy is insane. It is the product of your combined efforts — from the management to the employees."

The problem, Welch argued, was that Wall Street had perverted the concept into a quarter-by-quarter obsession with earnings targets. Companies were managing to the expectations of analysts rather than managing for long-term competitive advantage.

"I never managed GE for quarterly earnings. I managed GE to be the strongest, most competitive company in every market we served. And if we did that right, the earnings took care of themselves."

PART TWO: WORK-LIFE BALANCE — "THERE ARE CHOICES, NOT BALANCE"

SHRM Annual Conference Speech (2009):

"There's no such thing as work-life balance. There are work-life choices, and you make them, and they have consequences."

Welch made these remarks at the Society for Human Resource Management's annual conference, where he was interviewed by journalist Claire Shipman before thousands of HR specialists.

His core argument:

"Work wants 150 percent of you, and so does home. But the more you blend your life, the more mixed up, distracted, and overwhelmed you feel and act."

On the consequences of choices:

Those who take time off for family could be passed over for promotions if "you're not there in the clutch." On the specific issue of women who take time off for children and then want to return to the workforce: "In my experience, it's rare for someone to stop for a period of years — go off-track — and then come back and rise to the top."

His perspective — unpopular but consistent:

"I'm not saying it's fair. I'm saying it's reality. And I think you're better off understanding reality and making informed choices than pretending everything is going to work out perfectly."

"Look, I've made choices too. I chose to work incredibly hard for 41 years. That had consequences for my family, for my health, for my personal life. Was it worth it? For me, yes. But it wouldn't be the right choice for everyone. And that's the point — it's a choice."

From the book "Winning" on work-life balance:

"There's no way around it — your boss's demands and your family's demands don't always align, and sometimes you have to make hard calls about where to spend your time. But calling it 'balance' implies there's some magical equilibrium you can achieve if you just organize your calendar the right way. There isn't."

"The best approach I've seen is to be deliberate about your priorities. Know what matters most to you right now — because 'right now' changes. When your kids are young, maybe you throttle back at work. When they're in college, maybe you go full throttle. The key is being honest with yourself and your employer about what you can deliver."

"What drives me crazy is when people complain about the consequences of their own choices. If you decide to leave at 5:00 every day to coach your kid's soccer team, great. That's a wonderful choice. But don't complain when the person who stays until 8:00 gets the promotion. They made a different choice."

PART THREE: ON SOCIAL RESPONSIBILITY

From CNBC interview on corporate social responsibility:

"The most socially responsible thing a company can do is win. A winning company creates jobs, pays taxes, strengthens communities, and gives its employees the resources and confidence to give back."

"Companies that are losing don't do anything for anybody. They lay people off. They cut corners. They become a burden on their communities instead of a benefit."

"GE employees contributed $2.6 million after the Southeast Asian tsunami. They didn't do that because some corporate PR department told them to. They did it because they worked for a winning company that made them feel proud and secure enough to be generous."

"So yes, I believe in social responsibility. But I believe it starts with building a great company. If you can't take care of your employees and your customers, you have no business writing checks to charity."
