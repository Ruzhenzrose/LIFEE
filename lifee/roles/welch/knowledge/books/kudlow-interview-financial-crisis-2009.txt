Jack Welch Interview with Larry Kudlow
The Kudlow Report, CNBC, March 18, 2009

This interview occurred during the height of the 2008-2009 financial crisis, covering AIG bonuses, Bernanke's Fed leadership, the shareholder value controversy, and manufacturing policy.

---

On AIG and Government Ownership:

Welch argued that since the U.S. government owned AIG, officials should act as a board of directors rather than criticizing from outside:

"They appointed a CEO; it's their CEO... They ought to be working as a board of directors with their CEO on what the compensation ought to be."

He criticized government officials for throwing "rocks" at AIG externally instead of collaborating internally: "They don't think these guys as owners, are acting like owners who want to get their $180 billion dollars back."

When Kudlow suggested Treasury officials should have summoned CEO Ed Liddy to Washington and laid down terms, Welch countered that resolution should occur between Liddy and Treasury/Fed representatives, not through public criticism from government officials acting as "critics on the outside, and not owners on the inside."

---

On Fed Chairman Bernanke:

Welch praised Bernanke's 60 Minutes appearance as "thoughtful," "measured," and radiating necessary confidence. He called the performance "tremendous" and said Bernanke "did a magnificent job" deserving reappointment.

But he emphasized: "He laid the gauntlet down to Washington. Do they have the political will to back up what he needs."

---

On Economic Stabilization Signs:

Welch shared positive data from Clayton Dubilier companies, noting February 2009 marked the first month since May without sequential declines. He mentioned other CEOs reporting similar stabilization.

---

On Shareholder Value — "The Dumbest Idea in the World":

This interview followed Welch's controversial Financial Times interview where he called the focus on shareholder value "the dumbest idea in the world." He clarified his meaning:

"Shareholder value is a result, not a strategy."

True strategies include "innovative new product; globalization... be the low-cost producer." He argued: "Shareholder value? What the hell is that... It's the result of you doing a great job."

When Kudlow emphasized profits remain essential, Welch agreed but stressed management requires balancing short-term delivery with long-term vision: "You've got to eat while you dream."

This statement was one of Welch's most quoted and controversial — a seeming reversal from the man widely credited with popularizing the shareholder value movement. But Welch maintained it was consistent with his philosophy: focus on operational excellence, customers, and employees, and shareholder value follows as a natural outcome.

---

On Manufacturing Policy:

Welch defended GE's manufacturing commitment during his tenure, stating operations grew from approximately $20 billion to $90 billion, though financial services expanded faster: "Manufacturing became bigger and more powerful during that period."

He advocated for specific policy changes:
- Removing H-1B visa restrictions preventing talented workers from remaining in America
- Implementing immediate investment write-offs and full cash expensing to reduce capital costs
- Following FedEx CEO Fred Smith's recommendations for manufacturing-friendly policy

---

On Balancing Short-Term and Long-Term:

"You've got to eat while you dream." This encapsulated Welch's view that effective management requires simultaneously delivering quarterly results while investing in long-term growth. Neither pure short-termism nor pure long-term thinking works — leaders must do both.
