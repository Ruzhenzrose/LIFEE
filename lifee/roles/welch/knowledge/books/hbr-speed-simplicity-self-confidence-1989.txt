Speed, Simplicity, Self-Confidence: An Interview with Jack Welch
Harvard Business Review, September-October 1989
Interviewed by Noel Tichy and Ram Charan

On Leadership:
"Good business leaders create a vision, articulate the vision, passionately own the vision, and relentlessly drive it to completion." Welch emphasized that authentic leaders maintain accessibility and genuine communication rather than relying on formal channels.

On Organizational Candor:
Welch stressed the importance of "facing reality, seeing the world as it is rather than as you wish it were." He illustrated this principle through GE's locomotive business, which successfully pivoted when market conditions changed unexpectedly.

On Simplicity:
"For a large organization to be simple, its people must have self-confidence and intellectual self-assurance." Welch argued that complexity often stems from managerial insecurity rather than business necessity. Insecure managers create complexity. Frightened, nervous managers use thick, convoluted planning books and overflowing slide presentations full of everything they've known since childhood. Real leaders don't need clutter. People must have the self-confidence to be clear, precise, to be sure that every person in their organization—highest to lowest—understands what the business is trying to achieve. But it's not easy. You can't believe how hard it is for people to be simple, how much they fear being simple. They worry that if they're simple, people will think they're simpleminded. In reality, of course, it's just the reverse. Clear, tough-minded people are the most simple.

On Organizational Structure:
Welch eliminated management layers and expanded span of control, believing that "an overburdened, overstretched executive is the best executive." This forced delegation and reduced bureaucratic meddling. Every layer is a step away from the customer. We're trying to create an atmosphere where people can reach into the various businesses and departments and search for better ways of doing things. We want to be a company that is constantly renewing itself, shedding the past, adapting to change.

On Work-Out:
Work-Out is a fluid program, not a set of procedures. This initiative aimed to eliminate accumulated inefficiencies and foster direct communication between leaders and employees. Welch positioned it as fundamental to transforming GE's culture from control-oriented to empowerment-focused. The idea was resistance-free.Ural employees would come together in groups of 30 to 100 to share their views with their bosses—about how their business might be run better, about things their bosses were doing that slowed them down, about procedures that were needless. The boss was required to respond on the spot—yes, no, or "we'll get back to you by such and such a date."

On Psychological Contracts:
GE shifted from lifetime employment security to a merit-based relationship where "jobs at GE are the best in the world for people who are willing to compete. We have the best training and development resources and an environment committed to providing opportunities for personal and professional growth."

On Self-Confidence:
Self-confidence is the fuel of productivity and creativity, decisiveness and speed. Self-confidence does not grow in someone who is just told they are wonderful. Even in the best of companies, there are people who are overwhelmed. They have lost their self-confidence. They've been beaten down. They feel like victims. The way to turn this around is to give people a chance to do things they didn't think they could do—to surprise themselves, to raise their own level of aspirations.

On Bureaucracy:
Bureaucracy is terrified by speed and hates simplicity. It fosters defensiveness, intrigue, sometimes meanness. Those who are trapped in it are not big thinkers, nor are they dreamers or innovators. They're people who learned to survive in a bureaucracy. Bureaucracy strangles. Speed keeps people fresh. Speed makes them feel they are part of something alive.

On the Role of the CEO:
The CEO should be the person who sees the company as it really is and then acts on that reality. There's nothing so real as reality. The CEO is the reality-checker. People come in and present all kinds of rosy scenarios. The CEO has to cut through the static and ask: "But what's the reality?"
